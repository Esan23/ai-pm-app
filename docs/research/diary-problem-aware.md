# The Diary of Daniel Okafor — Problem Aware

*Persona-voice messaging asset. First-person, introspective. Emotional arc moves from the dominant negative emotions (overwhelm, dread, frustration) toward the resolution state (control, pride, relief). For mining VOC language, hook copy, and landing-page narrative.*

---

## Entry One — Before

**Tuesday. 2:47 a.m.**

I'm staring at the ceiling again. Same hairline crack over the closet I've been meaning to patch for two years. I track that crack better than I track my own projects, and somehow that's the thing that finally got me out of bed to write this down.

There are three AI projects in flight right now. *Three.* And if you put a gun to my head and asked me — not the leadership-deck version, the real version — where each one actually stands, I couldn't tell you. The truth is scattered across nine Slack threads, four ChatGPT tabs I'll never reopen, a Notion page that lied to me last week, a Jira board nobody believes anymore, and the inside of my own skull. The only place the project is *whole* is in my head, and my head is a terrible database. It doesn't back up. It crashes at 2:47 a.m.

I keep coming back to the thing nobody at work says out loud: it's happening again. The same quiet, creeping drift that killed the last one. Six months of good people doing real work, and at the end of it — nothing you could point to. No body, no autopsy, just a project that got thinner and thinner until one day it wasn't there and everyone pretended that was the plan. I felt that one in my chest for a month. I feel the early version of it now, low and electric, like a smoke detector chirping in another room.

Here's what I can't shake. I am a *good builder.* Give me a problem I can hold in code and I will out-ship anyone. But the moment the work stops being something I can type and starts being something I have to *coordinate* — who owns this, where does this live, what did Copilot actually finish versus what Gemini just hallucinated into a doc — I lose the thread. And I'm starting to suspect that's not a tooling problem. I'm starting to suspect it's *me.* That I'm a senior engineer cosplaying as a leader, and the gap is exactly the size of all the context I'm holding by hand.

Jira wasn't built for this. None of it was. It all assumes a tidy little sprint marching in a line, and AI work doesn't march, it *sprawls* — experiments, dead ends, a model that worked Tuesday and broke Thursday for reasons nobody logged. So I do the only thing I know how to do. I become the source of truth. I absorb it. I rebuild the whole picture in my head every single morning like Sisyphus with a standup.

It doesn't scale. I know it doesn't scale. I am the bottleneck and the backup plan and the single point of failure, and I am so tired.

Maya asked me tonight why I was working late *again.* I didn't have an answer that didn't sound pathetic. I wasn't even building. I was just... reconciling. Copy-pasting context into a chat window so an AI could give me back something I half-understood. That's not the job I thought I'd have. That's not the dad I want the kids to see on the trail Saturday — if I even make it to the trail Saturday.

I should sleep. I won't. The crack in the ceiling and I are going to wait this one out together.

## Entry Two — During (First Use)

**Two weeks later. Thursday, late afternoon. Coffee gone cold.**

I went in skeptical. Of course I did. Everything is "AI-powered" now — it's wallpaper, it's noise, it means nothing. I've watched a dozen tools promise to be the source of truth and become one more place where the truth goes to get out of date. So I came in arms crossed, already drafting the tweet about why it didn't work.

I dumped the chaos in. All three projects. The messy, real, embarrassing state of them — not the deck version, the *actual* version. And I waited for the thing to choke on it the way they always do.

It didn't choke.

What it did was quieter and, honestly, a little unnerving. It *assembled* it. Portfolio at the top, the projects under it, the user stories under those, the tasks under those — the hierarchy I've been holding in my head for months suddenly *outside* my head, sitting on a screen, legible. I scrolled the whole thing and I felt this strange loosening in my jaw, a muscle I didn't know I'd been clenching since Q1.

Then I saw the thing I didn't know I'd been starving for. Next to a finished story it told me *which* tool actually shipped it. Copilot opened that PR. That spec came out of a ChatGPT thread. That research Gemini ran — and it was sitting *next to the work*, attributed, tracked, not floating in some orphaned tab I'd lose by Friday. For the first time I could see the ground truth of my own project: not just what was done, but who — or what — did it.

I'm not going to pretend the cynic in me went silent. He's still here, arms still crossed, muttering *wait for the catch.* Part of me is braced for the moment it shows me a beautiful lie, the Notion graveyard in a nicer font. I clicked into a task half-expecting it to be stale and it just... wasn't. It knew. It had pulled the real status.

I sat back. The cold coffee. The 2:47 ceiling-crack feeling — the chirp in the other room — went quiet for the length of one long exhale. Not gone. Quiet. And I noticed how rare that is. How long it's been since I looked at my own work and felt *informed* instead of *behind.*

I'm not ready to say I trust it. I've been burned. But for the first time in months I looked at the truth of everything I'm responsible for and I didn't have to build that truth by hand. It was just *there.* And God, I didn't realize how heavy the by-hand version was until something else picked it up.

Good enough to keep going. We'll see if it holds.

## Entry Three — After

**Six weeks later. Saturday morning. On the porch before the trail.**

It held.

I almost don't know how to write a calm diary entry. I've gotten so used to journaling as a pressure valve — the 2:47 entries, the dread spilling out so I could function the next day — that sitting here with coffee that's actually still hot and *nothing screaming* feels suspicious. I keep waiting for the chirp. It doesn't come.

Monday I opened one view and saw the truth of every project in under a minute. Not my hand-built, willpower-fueled reconstruction. The real thing. Portfolio, projects, stories, tasks — and underneath each, the receipts: what Copilot built, what Claude drafted, what Gemini researched, all attributed, all current, all in one place that doesn't lie to me. The source of truth finally lives *outside* my skull. I am no longer the database. I am no longer the single point of failure. That sentence alone is worth more than the seat price.

And here's the part I didn't expect. When I stopped spending my mornings *reconciling*, I got the mornings back. I got the *work* back — the actual building, the thing I'm good at, the thing that made me want this career. I'm leading now instead of absorbing. When someone asks "who owns this?" I don't reach into my chest for it. I point at the screen.

The ghost of the dead project came up in a meeting this week — someone half-joked about it — and for the first time it didn't put a hand around my throat. Because I could see, with total clarity, that this one isn't drifting. It's not going to die quietly while I'm too buried to notice. I'd *see* the drift now. The system would show me before I felt it.

Maya noticed before I said anything. Said I seem like myself again. I didn't realize how far I'd traveled from *myself* until she handed it back to me as a compliment. I made the trail last Saturday. I'll make it this one. I'm writing this *because* I have the time, not because I can't sleep.

I used to be so afraid I was a builder pretending to be a leader, that the gap was the size of all the context I couldn't hold. Turns out the gap wasn't *me.* It was that I'd never had a system built for the way AI work actually moves — sprawling, multi-model, messy — instead of the tidy little sprint everyone else's tools imagined.

Calm competence. That's the thing I scribbled wanting, months ago, in a 2:47 entry I almost deleted out of embarrassment. High output. Present at home. The system holds it, not my stamina.

The ceiling crack is still there. I should patch it. But I'll do it Saturday — *after* the trail.
